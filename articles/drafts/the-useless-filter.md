# THE MOST USELESS INTERVIEW FILTER

If a company relies on LeetCode-style problems as the primary interview filter, I withdraw my candidacy.

Not because I can't solve them - but because they are a poor proxy for what I actually do as an engineer.

In real work, I design, implement, and test high-performance systems. I build across the stack - from kernel-level components close to the metal, up to user-facing applications. I've worked on greenfield systems and kept 20-year-old codebases alive. I understand internals of Linux, Windows, and macOS. I move between different services and languages when needed and make them talk to each other.

That's the job.

What I don't do is sit all day solving isolated puzzles with textbook data structures under artificial constraints.

## The Outcome

As a result of such practice, the industry experience side effect nobody talks about. This hiring pattern doesn't just filter candidates - it actively reshapes the entire learning ecosystem.

Students are rational. They optimize for what gets them hired.

So instead of building systems, experimenting with real technologies, or understanding how things work in practice, they grind endless DSA courses, competitive programming problems, obscure algorithm tricks.

Why? Because that's what Big Tech rewards.

As the result, fewer people interested in systems, tooling, infrastructure, building real projects, more people optimizing for interview performance instead of engineering ability.

We are literally training candidates to pass interviews - not to do the job.

And the industry preparing to "esoteric Big Tech interviews" is thriving, literally parasiting on engineering candidates.

## Raise the Bar

The "basic set" also doesn't stay stable.

When everyone learns the minimal reasonable skillset (heap, tree, hash table), the bar doesn't stay there. It escalates.

What used to be a signal of problem-solving ability becomes rare or esoteric data structures, tricky edge-case puzzles, solutions that rely on memorization rather than problem-solving.

At some point, it stops measuring understanding and starts measuring how much time you've invested in a very specific game.

## The Experience Paradox

And here's the part that makes even less sense.

If I want to pass these interviews, I'm expected to pause actual engineering work, stop building real systems, re-learn academic material in an artificial format, and compete directly with fresh graduates who just studied it.

In other words, the more real-world experience you have, the less aligned you are with the interview format.

## The Conclusion

I honestly don't know where this practice originally came from.

But today, it looks like a textbook case of "mindlessly do it because everyone else does"

It persists not because it's effective - but because it's normalized. If that's the primary filter, I'm out. Because I'd rather spend my time being a better engineer than a better contestant.
