## WFH: ADVANTAGES ARE OBVIOUS, LET'S TALK ABOUT PROBLEMS

Remote work saved companies during the pandemic and became a permanent part of our industry. But after several years of working remotely in different teams, I started noticing a number of problems that rarely appear in the optimistic narratives about WFH.

Below are some observations from my own experience - which strongly overlaps with discussions many engineers have been having since the transition to remore workspace.

1. Communication becomes weaker.
Text chat, voice calls, even video meetings are simply not as effective as talking in person. Ironically, video calls often become the opposite of efficient communication - long meetings consuming large portions of the workday with little outcome. Many engineers noticed the same thing: remote work tends to increase formal meetings while decreasing real understanding. Speaker assumes that audience understood, while they didn't. Non-verbal communication matters too.

2. Mentoring and knowledge transfer suffer.
In an office environment junior developers learn constantly - by overhearing discussions, asking quick questions, or observing how others approach problems. In remote teams that organic flow almost disappears. New employees often feel isolated and abandoned. In AI age chatbot often closes the gap, and in 90% of cases does this incorrectly.

3. Trust and team cohesion decline.
After several years of remote work in two different companies I noticed something unusual: relationships remained polite but cold. In office environments trust grows naturally - over coffee, hallway conversations, or simply sharing the same physical space.

4. Creativity drops when informal interaction disappears.
Many technology companies historically invested heavily in shared spaces and informal environments because spontaneous conversations often generate ideas. Remote work removes these accidental collisions between people - and with them many creative sparks.

5. Home slowly turns into the office.
This was the biggest surprise to me personally. When your workplace is inside your home, switching between "work mode" and "life mode" becomes difficult. Work leaks into evenings and weekends. Instead of freeing time, remote work can blur the boundary completely. Surprisingly became harder to spend time with the family. We were going out more often when I worked in the office.

6. And for many people there is another issue rarely discussed: loneliness. For employees living alone, work is often the last daily social connection. Removing that connection can have serious psychological consequences.

## WHAT TO DO AND HOW TO LIVE

Despite these problems and Big Tech companies returnong staff to office, remote work is here to stay. The goal is not to reject it, but to structure it properly.

1. Separate workspace from living space.
Never work in the same place where you relax or spend time with family. If possible, dedicate a separate room or consider coworking spaces. Explain your kids that daddy must not be disturbed in this time.

2. Maintain strict start and end times.
Without discipline the workday easily stretches into late evening. A defined schedule protects both productivity and family life.

3. Prefer richer communication channels.
Video calls are usually better than chat or voice. Seeing colleagues improves understanding and emotional connection.

4. Maintain personal discipline.
Even small things help - wearing business casual instead of "home mode", keeping a daily routine, creating clear mental transitions between work and rest.

5. Accept the trade-offs and adapt - as engineer supposed to do during any paradigmal shift, no matter it is technology or workflow.
Remote work saves commuting time and offers flexibility. But it requires conscious structure to avoid the hidden costs.

WFH is neither a miracle nor a disaster. Like many technological shifts in our industry, it simply requires new habits and new discipline. The best future probably lies somewhere in the middle - a balanced hybrid model combining flexibility with real human interaction.
