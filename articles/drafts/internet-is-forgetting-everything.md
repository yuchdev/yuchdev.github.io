Whoever said "nothing ever disappears on the internet" is either lying, or living in some parallel version of it.

Because in the real internet, everything disappears. Relentlessly. Irreversibly.

We're living through a strange period where things don't just change - they steadily degrade. Gradually, then suddenly. Systems become worse, interfaces more hostile, incentives more misaligned. There's no reason the internet would be immune to that - and it isn't.

I still remember what the internet felt like 15 years ago. It wasn't perfect, but it worked.

Google used to be a tool for finding information, not a maze designed to trap you in SEO sludge and ads. It had a clean interface, real signal in search results, useful analytics, cached pages that actually helped recover lost content- and didn't feel like a surveillance product first and a search sengine second.

YouTube didn't behave like a paranoid censor with a constantly shifting rulebook created in North Korean GULAG. Videos that had been perfectly fine for a decade didn't suddenly vanish because they became "offensive" to someone, somewhere, under rules nobody can clearly explain. Entire channels on humam history, harmless humor, customers rights and privacy just disappear overnight with no meaningful recourse.

Over the years, an enormous amount of information has simply vanished from the internet - completely and without a trace.
Entire websites. Articles. Forums. Videos. Communities collapse and take decades of accumulated knowledge with them.
The same applies to software, niche research, independent blogs, educatiinal content, entire creative ecosystems. 
Not hidden. Not deprecated. Gone.

Sometimes it's censorship.
Sometimes it's corporate greed.
Sometimes it's just neglect - domains expire, funding dries up, ownership changes.

Entire websites vanish when funding dries up.
Articles disappear after acquisitions.

For example, you have companies like Nintendo, aggressively erasing their own legacy from existence with the determination of someone trying to erase evidence, not preserve history. "We don't sell it anymore - and you're not allowed to have it either."

There are books you literally cannot buy anymore - at any price.
Your only option is to "borrow" them through Internet Archive, assuming they're even available - and even then, access is restricted, temporary, and fragile. You're not allowed to preserve them yourself.

There are films - actual pieces of cultural history - that exist only on torrent trackers.
Not because people are pirating out of convenience, but because there is no legal way to obtain them. No streaming, no physical copies, no store, nothing. And when the last seeders disappear, that film effectively ceases to exist.

Gone - not philosophically, but practically.

A shocking amount of internet content exists in exactly one place at any given time. When it's deleted, it's gone forever. I could list a dozen examples of lost material Ivdeeply regret, without even trying hard.

People like to pretend there's a safety net. That the web "archives itself." That someone, somewhere, is keeping a copy.

They point to Internet Archive like it's a backup of reality.

It isn't.

It misses huge portions of the web.
It struggles with modern JS - heavy sites.
It often fails to preserve video or interactive content.
Sometimes it captures nothing but an empty shell.

And even when it works, it's partial, inconsistent, and one policy change away from losing access itself.

So I've adjusted accordingly.

I save videos and audio I know I might need later.
And I'm moving toward something more systematic: scraping and indexing content that actually matters to me, content with long - term value, content that shouldn't depend on whether a company pivots, a server bill goes unpaid, or a moderation policy shifts. I implement indexing and MCP server allowing search and AI utilization.

Because the myth isn't that "nothing disappears."
The myth is that someone else will preserve it for you.
