Kids are incredible.

They learn things frighteningly fast. Give a child a phone and in minutes they know how to install apps, skip ads, and find exactly what they want.

They are equally good at two things:
Learning technology - and hiding how they use it from parents.

So when we hand them a smartphone as the universal answer to curiosity, we shouldn't be surprised when they master it faster than we understand the consequences.

Now governments are trying to fix those consequences.

France talks about banning social media under 15. Others are following.

As a parent - I understand the instinct.
As an engineer - I have one question:

HOW EXACTLY YOU ENFORCE IT?

There are only a few real options:

- Self-declared age - already useless ("born in 1998" works perfectly)
- ID verification - effective, but kills anonymity
- Device-level controls - easy to bypass
- ISP blocking - welcome to the VPN era

The only solution that actually works at scale is identity verification.

And that means:

– centralized databases
– biometric data
– long-term traceability
– a very attractive target for breaches

In other words, we solve one problem by quietly introducing another.

And there's a second-order effect nobody talks about.

When something becomes forbidden, it becomes attractive.

Teenagers don't disappear from social networks. They migrate:

Snapchat → Telegram → Discord → private servers

Less visible. Less moderated. More risky.

Add a bit of creativity and you also get:

– burner devices
– shared accounts
– preconfigured VPN phones

We've seen this pattern before. Prohibition doesn't eliminate behavior - it reshapes it.

And the popular analogy with alcohol doesn't really hold.

Alcohol regulation controls a transaction.
Social media regulation controls access to speech and interaction.

That's a very different category.

So the real question isn't:

DO WE WANT TO PROTECT CHILDREN?

Of course we do.

The real question is:

WHAT ARE WE WILLING TO SACRIFICE TO DO IT?

Because if the answer is:

– digital ID
– biometric verification
– centralized access control

Then we are not just protecting kids - we protecting these vulnerable and centralized systems.
And we are protecting our right to privacy (Granted to you by the UN Declaration of Human Rights)
And we are changing how the internet works.

Of course, as a father of a 4-year-old, I want safety.

But as someone who builds systems, I also know:

THE ARCHITECTURE INTRODUCED TO PROTECT CHILDREN WON'T STAY LIMITED TO CHILDREN

Maybe the answer is less about prohibition - and more about friction:

– safer algorithms
– better parental tools
– education
– delayed exposure

Because once you redesign the foundation, you don't easily go back.
